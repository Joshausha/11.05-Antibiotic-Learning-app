## 🔴 TIER 1 - CRITICAL RELATIONSHIPS (Cards 1-8)

### Card 1: Neonatal Fever - Most Important Bacterial Pathogens

**FRONT**: What are the two most important bacterial pathogens in neonatal fever? What is the empiric antibiotic regimen?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:
1. **Group B Streptococcus (GBS)**
2. **Escherichia coli**

**Empiric Regimen**: **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** (or Ampicillin + Cefotaxime)

**Why This Combination?**:
- **Ampicillin** covers GBS and Enterococcus (Gram-positive)
- **Gentamicin** covers E. coli and Gram-negatives
- Together they cover the two most important neonatal pathogens

**When to Add Acyclovir**:
- Seizures
- CSF pleocytosis
- Skin vesicles
- Hepatitis
- Maternal HSV history

**Board Pearl**: This is THE classic neonatal empiric regimen - know it cold!

</details>

### Card 2: Acute Otitis Media - The "Big 3"

**FRONT**: What are the three most common bacterial pathogens for pediatric acute otitis media (AOM)?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER** - The "Big 3":
1. **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (Pneumococcus) - most common
2. **Haemophilus influenzae** (non-typeable) - second most common
3. **Moraxella catarrhalis** - third most common

**First-Line Treatment**:
- **Amoxicillin** (high-dose: 80-90 mg/kg/day)

**When to Use Amoxicillin-Clavulanate**:
- Treatment failure at 48-72 hours
- Severe disease at presentation
- Recent antibiotic use (<30 days)

**Key Fact**: M. catarrhalis produces β-lactamase **100% of the time** (why amox-clav works for treatment failures)

**Board Pearl**: Memorize "Pneumo, H. flu, Moraxella" for AOM/sinusitis

</details>

### Card 3: Skin Infections - Purulent vs. Non-Purulent

**FRONT**: What is the most important clinical distinction in pediatric skin and soft tissue infections? How does this guide antibiotic selection?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**CRITICAL DISTINCTION**: **Purulent vs. Non-Purulent**

**PURULENT** (Abscesses, Furuncles):
- **Primary Pathogen**: Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA)
- **PRIMARY Treatment**: Incision & Drainage (I&D)
- **Antibiotics** (if needed): TMP-SMX, Clindamycin, or Doxycycline
- **MRSA coverage**: YES

**NON-PURULENT** (Cellulitis):
- **Primary Pathogen**: Group A Streptococcus >> Staph aureus
- **PRIMARY Treatment**: Antibiotics (I&D not applicable)
- **Antibiotics**: Cephalexin or Dicloxacillin
- **MRSA coverage**: Usually NO

**Key Teaching Point**: I&D is DEFINITIVE therapy for abscesses. Antibiotics are adjunctive and often not needed for simple abscesses.

**Board Pearl**: "Purulent = MRSA = I&D first; Non-purulent = GAS = Cephalexin"

</details>

### Card 4: Pediatric Septic Arthritis - Age-Specific Pathogen

**FRONT**: A 20-month-old refuses to bear weight on his right leg with a swollen, warm knee. What age-specific pathogen MUST you cover empirically that is NOT covered by clindamycin?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Kingella kingae**

**Why K. kingae Matters**:
- **Critical pathogen in children <5 years** (especially 6-36 months)
- Accounts for up to 50% of culture-positive septic arthritis in this age
- Fastidious organism (difficult to culture) → often "culture-negative"
- **NOT covered by clindamycin** (common MRSA agent)
- **NOT reliably susceptible to oxacillin/nafcillin**

**Most Common Pathogens by Age**:
- **<5 years**: Staph aureus > K. kingae >> Group A Strep
- **≥5 years**: Staph aureus >> Group A Strep

**Empiric Regimen for Children <5 Years**:
- **Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone** (covers MRSA + K. kingae)
- OR Nafcillin/Cefazolin (covers MSSA + K. kingae)

**Board Pearl**: "Kingella kingae is THE pediatric-specific bone/joint pathogen. Clindamycin misses it!"

</details>

### Card 5: Intra-Abdominal Infections - Polymicrobial Coverage

**FRONT**: A 9-year-old with perforated appendicitis is going to the OR. What TWO categories of organisms must your empiric antibiotics cover?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **AEROBIC Gram-Negatives + ANAEROBES**

**AEROBIC Gram-Negative Pathogens**:
- E. coli (most common)
- Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Proteus

**ANAEROBIC Pathogens**:
- Bacteroides fragilis (most important)
- Other anaerobes

**Empiric Antibiotic Options**:

**Single-Agent** (covers both):
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam** (Zosyn) - first-line

**Dual-Agent** (aerobic + anaerobic):
- **Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole**

**Why Both Are Needed**:
- Aerobic Gram-negatives → peritonitis and sepsis
- Anaerobes → abscess formation

**Critical Teaching Point**: "Source control (surgery) is ESSENTIAL. No amount of antibiotics will cure an undrained abscess."

**Board Pearl**: "Intra-abdominal = E. coli + Bacteroides = Piperacillin-tazobactam OR Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole"

</details>

### Card 6: Bacterial Meningitis - Post-Hib Vaccine Era

**FRONT**: In a fully vaccinated 6-year-old with bacterial meningitis, what are the two most likely pathogens? What is the empiric antibiotic regimen?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER** - The Post-Hib Vaccine "Big 2":
1. **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (Pneumococcus) - most common
2. **Neisseria meningitidis** (Meningococcus) - second most common

*H. influenzae type B is now rare in vaccinated populations*

**Empiric Regimen**: **Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone** (or Cefotaxime)

**Why This Combination?**:
- **Ceftriaxone**: covers pneumococcus, meningococcus, H. flu
- **Vancomycin**: covers cephalosporin-resistant pneumococcus
- **NEVER use ceftriaxone alone** for empiric bacterial meningitis

**Additional Therapy**:
- **Dexamethasone**: 0.15 mg/kg q6h × 2-4 days (before or with first antibiotic dose)

**Public Health Action for Meningococcal Disease**:
- Close contact prophylaxis (rifampin, ciprofloxacin, or ceftriaxone)
- Report to health department immediately

**Board Pearl**: "Hib vaccine eliminated H. flu type B meningitis. Pneumococcus and meningococcus now dominate."

</details>

### Card 7: Acute Sinusitis - Same Trio as AOM

**FRONT**: What three bacterial pathogens cause acute bacterial sinusitis in children? What is the first-line antibiotic?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER** - The "Big 3" (Same as AOM):
1. **Streptococcus pneumoniae**
2. **Haemophilus influenzae** (non-typeable)
3. **Moraxella catarrhalis**

**First-Line Treatment**:
- **Amoxicillin** OR **Amoxicillin-clavulanate**
- High-dose amoxicillin: 80-90 mg/kg/day

**When to Use Amoxicillin-Clavulanate**:
- Severe disease
- Recent antibiotic use
- Treatment failure

**Duration**:
- 10 days (children <2 years)
- 5-7 days (children ≥2 years)

**Anatomic Connection**:
- Same organisms cause AOM, sinusitis, and occasionally pneumonia
- All colonize upper respiratory tract
- All can cause bacterial superinfection after viral URI

**Board Pearl**: "If you know the AOM trio, you know the sinusitis trio - they're the same!"

</details>

### Card 8: Upper Respiratory Anatomy Connection

**FRONT**: Why do Haemophilus influenzae and Moraxella catarrhalis co-occur in both acute otitis media and acute sinusitis?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Anatomic-Association Relationship**

**Why These Pathogens Go Together**:
1. **Anatomic proximity**: Sinuses → middle ear → lower respiratory tract (all connected)
2. **Colonization**: Both colonize the upper respiratory tract (nasopharynx)
3. **Post-viral superinfection**: Both cause bacterial superinfection after viral URI
4. **Shared empiric coverage**: Amoxicillin or amoxicillin-clavulanate covers both

**β-Lactamase Production**:
- H. influenzae: ~30-50% produce β-lactamase
- M. catarrhalis: **100% produce β-lactamase**
- This is why amoxicillin-clavulanate is needed for treatment failures

**Clinical Teaching Point**:
These organisms don't just randomly co-occur. They share the same anatomic sites and have similar pathogenic mechanisms.

**Board Pearl**: "Amoxicillin-clavulanate needed when β-lactamase producers (H. flu, M. cat) suspected"

</details>

## 🟠 TIER 2 - HIGH PRIORITY RELATIONSHIPS (Cards 9-20)

### Card 9: Urinary Tract Infections - E. coli Dominance

**FRONT**: What percentage of pediatric UTIs are caused by E. coli? What other organism should you consider in neonates that is NOT covered by cephalosporins?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:
- **E. coli accounts for 75-90% of all pediatric UTIs**
- In neonates, also consider: **Enterococcus**

**Critical Gap**: **Cephalosporins do NOT cover Enterococcus**

**Why This Matters in Neonates**:
- Neonatal UTI empiric regimen: Ampicillin + Gentamicin (or Cefotaxime)
- Ampicillin covers Enterococcus (cephalosporins don't)
- This is another reason why "Ampicillin + Gentamicin" is used in neonatal fever

**Other UTI Pathogens** (less common):
- Klebsiella (3-5%)
- Proteus (5-10%) - associated with staghorn calculi (urease production)
- S. saprophyticus (10-20% in sexually active adolescent females)

**Board Pearl**: "If you're guessing one UTI pathogen, guess E. coli. If it's a neonate, don't forget Enterococcus (ampicillin required)."

</details>

### Card 10: Community-Acquired Pneumonia - Typical vs. Atypical

**FRONT**: A 9-year-old with pneumonia has gradual onset, dry cough, and interstitial infiltrates. What "atypical" pathogen should you cover in addition to pneumococcus?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Mycoplasma pneumoniae**

**Typical vs. Atypical Pneumonia**:

**"Typical" Pneumonia** (Pneumococcus):
- Lobar consolidation
- High fever, productive cough
- Responds to β-lactams

**"Atypical" Pneumonia** (Mycoplasma):
- Interstitial infiltrates
- Gradual onset, dry cough
- "Walking pneumonia"
- Responds to macrolides or doxycycline
- **β-lactams ineffective** (no cell wall)

**Age Considerations**:
- Mycoplasma more common in school-aged children and adolescents
- Pneumococcus remains #1 bacterial CAP across all ages

**Empiric Therapy for School-Age/Adolescents**:
- **Amoxicillin** (pneumococcus) + **Azithromycin** (Mycoplasma)
- Dual therapy covers both typical and atypical

**Board Pearl**: "Classic dichotomy of typical vs. atypical, but clinical overlap is significant. In school-age kids, consider dual therapy."

</details>

### Card 11: Post-Influenza Pneumonia - Bacterial Superinfection

**FRONT**: A 7-year-old had influenza last week. Now presents with severe pneumonia and cavitary lesions on CXR. What bacterial pathogen should you suspect and cover empirically?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Staphylococcus aureus** (including MRSA)

**Post-Influenza Bacterial Superinfection**:
- Influenza and respiratory viruses predispose to bacterial pneumonia
- Staph aureus and pneumococcus are most common
- Staph aureus can cause severe necrotizing pneumonia
- Cavitary lesions, pleural effusion suggest Staph aureus

**Empiric Therapy for Severe CAP with MRSA Concern**:
- **Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin** (or Clindamycin)
- Ceftriaxone: pneumococcal coverage
- Vancomycin: MRSA coverage

**When to Add MRSA Coverage in CAP**:
- Post-influenza bacterial superinfection
- Cavitary lesions or necrotizing features
- Pleural effusion/empyema
- MRSA colonization or recent infection

**Historical Context**: 1918 influenza pandemic - most deaths from bacterial superinfection (Staph aureus, pneumococcus)

**Board Pearl**: "Post-flu pneumonia = think Staph aureus (especially MRSA). Add vancomycin to empiric regimen."

</details>

### Card 12: Retropharyngeal Abscess - Polymicrobial Nature

**FRONT**: Why are retropharyngeal abscesses polymicrobial? What single antibiotic covers both the aerobic and anaerobic components?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Oral flora contains both aerobes and anaerobes**

**Common Pathogens in Retropharyngeal Abscess**:
- **Aerobes**: Group A Strep, Staph aureus, S. anginosus group
- **Anaerobes**: Fusobacterium, Bacteroides, Prevotella, Peptostreptococcus

**Single-Agent Empiric Therapy**: **Ampicillin-Sulbactam** (Unasyn)
- Ampicillin: aerobic Strep coverage
- Sulbactam: β-lactamase inhibition + anaerobic coverage

**Alternative**: **Clindamycin**
- Covers oral flora (aerobes + anaerobes)
- Also covers MSSA and many MRSA (if susceptible)

**Critical Treatment**:
- **Surgical drainage essential** (I&D)
- Antibiotics alone will fail
- Airway management critical (may need intubation)

**Board Pearl**: "Head/neck abscesses = oral flora = polymicrobial = ampicillin-sulbactam or clindamycin"

</details>

### Card 13: MRSA Resistance Mechanism

**FRONT**: What genetic mechanism causes methicillin resistance in Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)? What antibiotic is universally effective against MRSA?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:
- **Genetic Mechanism**: **mecA gene** → encodes **PBP2a** (altered penicillin-binding protein)
- **Universal Treatment**: **Vancomycin**

**Resistance Mechanism**:
- mecA gene → PBP2a protein
- PBP2a has low affinity for β-lactam antibiotics
- All β-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) ineffective

**MRSA Categories**:
- **CA-MRSA** (Community-Associated): skin/soft tissue infections
- **HA-MRSA** (Healthcare-Associated): bloodstream, pneumonia, surgical sites

**Treatment Options for MRSA**:
- **Vancomycin**: universal coverage (all MRSA susceptible)
- **Clindamycin**: if susceptible (good oral bioavailability)
- **Linezolid**: alternative (also covers VRE)
- **TMP-SMX, Doxycycline**: outpatient SSTI

**Related Organism**: Coagulase-negative Staph (CoNS) also has mecA-mediated resistance (same mechanism)

**Board Pearl**: "mecA = MRSA. All β-lactams fail. Vancomycin always works."

</details>

### Card 14: ESBL Organisms - When to Use Carbapenems

**FRONT**: Your patient has a UTI with E. coli resistant to ceftriaxone. Culture shows "ESBL-producing organism." What antibiotic class should you use?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Carbapenems** (Meropenem or Ertapenem)

**ESBL = Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamases**

**What ESBLs Resist**:
- Penicillins (including with β-lactamase inhibitors)
- 1st, 2nd, 3rd generation cephalosporins
- Aztreonam
- **NOT Carbapenems** ✅

**Common ESBL Organisms**:
- E. coli
- Klebsiella species
- Other Enterobacterales

**Risk Factors for ESBL**:
- Healthcare exposure
- Prior antibiotic use (especially cephalosporins)
- International travel
- Nursing home residence

**Empiric Carbapenem Therapy**:
- **Ertapenem**: community-acquired infections
- **Meropenem**: healthcare-associated + Pseudomonas coverage

**Board Pearl**: "ESBL organisms resist cephalosporins but NOT carbapenems. Think meropenem or ertapenem."

</details>

### Card 15: VRE - Enterococcus Species Distinction

**FRONT**: What is the key difference in antibiotic susceptibility between Enterococcus faecalis and Enterococcus faecium?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:
- **E. faecalis**: Usually **ampicillin-susceptible**
- **E. faecium**: Often **ampicillin-resistant** AND **vancomycin-resistant** (VRE)

**Treatment Implications**:
- **E. faecalis infection**: Ampicillin (first-line)
- **E. faecium infection**: Vancomycin (if susceptible) OR Linezolid/Daptomycin (if VRE)

**VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus)**:
- Primarily E. faecium
- Healthcare-associated infections
- Resistance via vanA or vanB genes
- Requires linezolid or daptomycin

**Clinical Context**:
- Both cause bloodstream infections, UTIs, intra-abdominal infections
- E. faecium more often resistant and healthcare-associated
- Species identification essential for appropriate therapy

**Infection Control**:
- VRE requires contact precautions
- Prevent transmission in healthcare settings

**Board Pearl**: "E. faecalis = ampicillin. E. faecium = VRE risk = linezolid/daptomycin if resistant."

</details>

### Card 16: C. difficile Risk - Treatment Interaction

**FRONT**: What class of antibiotics most commonly causes C. difficile infection? What is the mechanism?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:
- **Highest Risk**: Cephalosporins, Fluoroquinolones, Clindamycin
- **Mechanism**: Disruption of normal gut flora → C. diff overgrowth and toxin production

**How It Happens**:
1. Broad-spectrum antibiotic disrupts normal colonic flora
2. C. difficile spores germinate (resistant to disruption)
3. C. diff proliferates without competition
4. Produces toxins A and B
5. Result: antibiotic-associated diarrhea, colitis

**High-Risk Antibiotics**:
- 3rd generation cephalosporins (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime)
- Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin)
- Clindamycin
- Broad-spectrum penicillins (piperacillin-tazobactam)

**Treatment of C. diff**:
1. **STOP offending antibiotic** (if possible)
2. **Start C. diff therapy**: Oral vancomycin (first-line) OR Fidaxomicin

**Prevention**:
- Antibiotic stewardship (use narrowest spectrum, shortest duration)
- Hand hygiene with soap and water (alcohol doesn't kill spores)

**Board Pearl**: "Broad-spectrum antibiotics (especially cephalosporins) → C. diff risk. First step in treatment: STOP the offending antibiotic."

</details>

### Card 17: Proteus in UTI - Staghorn Calculi

**FRONT**: A child with recurrent UTIs grows Proteus mirabilis. What complication should you screen for? What is the mechanism?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Staghorn Calculi** (Struvite Stones)

**Mechanism**:
- Proteus produces **urease enzyme**
- Urease converts urea → ammonia
- Ammonia alkalinizes urine (high pH)
- Alkaline urine → precipitation of magnesium ammonium phosphate (struvite)
- Result: large staghorn calculi filling renal pelvis

**Clinical Clues**:
- Alkaline urine pH (>7.5)
- "Triple phosphate crystals" on urinalysis
- Recurrent UTIs
- Imaging shows large branching calculi

**Management**:
- Treat acute UTI (cephalosporins cover Proteus)
- Evaluate for anatomic abnormalities (VUR, obstruction)
- Surgical removal of stones may be required
- Prevent recurrence (prophylactic antibiotics, urologic evaluation)

**Other UTI Associations**:
- E. coli: most common (75-90%)
- Klebsiella: less common
- Proteus: 5-10%, associated with stones and anatomic abnormalities

**Board Pearl**: "Proteus UTI = urease production = alkaline urine = staghorn calculi. Evaluate for anatomic abnormalities."

</details>

### Card 18: Neonatal HSV - When to Add Acyclovir

**FRONT**: A 10-day-old presents with fever and seizures. CSF shows WBC 45 (mostly lymphocytes), protein 120, glucose normal. What pathogen should you cover in addition to bacterial meningitis?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)** - Add **Acyclovir**

**High-Risk Features for Neonatal HSV**:
- **Seizures** ⚠️ (major red flag)
- **CSF pleocytosis** (especially lymphocytic)
- **Skin vesicles** (but absence doesn't exclude HSV!)
- **Hepatitis** (elevated transaminases)
- **Maternal HSV history**

**"Rule of 7s" for Neonatal HSV**:
- Peak incidence at **7 days** of life
- CSF WBC often **<7** (or lymphocyte-predominant)
- Can present with fever alone (no classic features)

**Empiric Therapy for High-Risk Neonatal Fever**:
- **Ampicillin + Gentamicin + Acyclovir** (triple therapy)
- Don't wait for CSF results if high clinical suspicion

**Consequences of Missing HSV**:
- Encephalitis → neurologic devastation
- Disseminated disease → multi-organ failure
- High mortality and morbidity

**Board Pearl**: "Neonatal seizures + fever = HSV until proven otherwise. Add acyclovir empirically."

</details>

### Card 19: Group A Strep vs. Staph aureus in SSTI

**FRONT**: How do you clinically distinguish infections caused by Group A Streptococcus vs. Staphylococcus aureus in skin infections?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Purulent vs. Non-Purulent**

**Group A Streptococcus (GAS)**:
- **Non-purulent cellulitis**
- Spreads along tissue planes (no abscess)
- Erythema, warmth, edema
- No localized collection of pus
- Treatment: β-lactams (cephalexin, dicloxacillin)

**Staphylococcus aureus (Including MRSA)**:
- **Purulent infections** (abscesses, furuncles, carbuncles)
- Localized collection of pus
- Fluctuant mass
- Requires incision & drainage (I&D)
- Treatment: I&D ± antibiotics (TMP-SMX, clindamycin if antibiotics needed)

**Key Clinical Distinction**:
- If there's a **drainable collection** → think Staph aureus → I&D primary therapy
- If it's **spreading cellulitis** without collection → think GAS → antibiotics primary therapy

**Severe Disease**:
- Both can cause severe invasive disease
- GAS: necrotizing fasciitis, toxic shock syndrome (exotoxin-mediated)
- Staph: necrotizing pneumonia, toxic shock syndrome (toxin-mediated)

**Board Pearl**: "Purulent = Staph = I&D. Non-purulent = GAS = Cephalexin."

</details>

### Card 20: S. anginosus Group - Abscess Former

**FRONT**: What is unique about Streptococcus anginosus group (milleri group) compared to other viridans streptococci?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Tendency to Form Abscesses**

**S. anginosus Group Characteristics**:
- Part of viridans streptococci (normal oral flora)
- Also called "milleri group" or "S. intermedius group"
- Includes: S. anginosus, S. constellatus, S. intermedius
- **Unique feature**: causes abscesses when translocated to deep spaces

**Common Infections**:
- Retropharyngeal abscess
- Peritonsillar abscess
- Parapharyngeal abscess
- Brain abscess
- Liver abscess
- Endocarditis (in high-risk patients)

**Polymicrobial Patterns**:
- Often co-infects with anaerobes (Fusobacterium, Bacteroides)
- Oral flora translocation → deep space infection

**Treatment**:
- **Surgical drainage** essential (abscesses)
- **Antibiotics**: Ampicillin-sulbactam or Clindamycin
- Covers both S. anginosus and anaerobic co-pathogens

**Mnemonic**: "**Anginosus = Abscesses**"

**Board Pearl**: "S. anginosus group is normal oral flora that causes abscesses when it gets to deep spaces. Think polymicrobial infection requiring drainage."

</details>

## 🟡 TIER 3 - MEDIUM PRIORITY RELATIONSHIPS (Cards 21-30)

### Card 21: S. saprophyticus - The Exception to CoNS

**FRONT**: A 16-year-old sexually active female has a UTI. Urine culture grows "Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus." Should you dismiss this as a contaminant?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **NO - Consider Staphylococcus saprophyticus**

**S. saprophyticus Characteristics**:
- Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus (CoNS)
- **Second most common cause of UTI in adolescent/young adult females** (after E. coli)
- Accounts for 10-20% of UTIs in this population
- **Exception to "CoNS = contaminant" rule**

**Clinical Context**:
- Sexually active adolescent females
- Community-acquired UTI
- True uropathogen (not a contaminant)

**Treatment**:
- Susceptible to most oral agents
- β-lactams (cephalexin)
- Nitrofurantoin
- TMP-SMX
- *May be resistant to fosfomycin in some regions*

**When to Suspect Contamination** (Other CoNS species):
- Staph epidermidis (most common CoNS)
- Multiple organisms in culture
- Low colony count
- No pyuria on urinalysis

**Board Pearl**: "S. saprophyticus is the exception: CoNS that's a TRUE uropathogen in adolescent females."

</details>

### Card 22: Klebsiella and Enterobacter - CRE Risk

**FRONT**: What does "CRE" stand for? Why are these organisms called "nightmare bacteria"?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **CRE = Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacterales**

**Why "Nightmare Bacteria" (CDC Designation)**:
- Resistant to carbapenems (last-line Gram-negative agents)
- Resistance via carbapenemase enzymes (KPC, NDM, OXA)
- Very limited treatment options
- High mortality rates
- Healthcare-associated transmission risk

**Common CRE Organisms**:
- Klebsiella pneumoniae (most common)
- Enterobacter species
- E. coli (less common)

**Treatment Options** (Limited):
- Polymyxins (colistin) - nephrotoxic
- Tigecycline - limited indications
- New β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitors (ceftazidime-avibactam, meropenem-vaborbactam)
- Combination therapy often required
- **Infectious disease consultation ESSENTIAL**

**Prevention**:
- Antibiotic stewardship
- Infection control (contact precautions)
- Prevent healthcare-associated transmission

**Related**: Enterobacter also has inducible AmpC β-lactamase (cephalosporin resistance develops during therapy)

**Board Pearl**: "CRE = carbapenem-resistant = nightmare bacteria = ID consult required"

</details>

### Card 23: Pneumococcus and Meningococcus - Meningitis Duo

**FRONT**: Why do you need to add vancomycin to ceftriaxone for empiric bacterial meningitis? Doesn't ceftriaxone cover pneumococcus?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Pneumococcal Cephalosporin Resistance**

**Why Vancomycin is Added**:
- Some pneumococcal strains are cephalosporin-resistant
- Resistance via altered penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs)
- Vancomycin provides additional coverage for resistant strains
- **Never use ceftriaxone alone for empiric meningitis**

**Empiric Bacterial Meningitis Regimen**:
- **Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone** (or Cefotaxime)

**What Each Drug Covers**:
- **Ceftriaxone**: Pneumococcus (most strains), Meningococcus, H. flu
- **Vancomycin**: Resistant pneumococcus, MRSA (if suspected)

**De-escalation Based on Susceptibilities**:
- If pneumococcus fully susceptible → can switch to ceftriaxone alone
- If meningococcus isolated → switch to ceftriaxone alone (universally susceptible)

**Dosing Considerations**:
- Higher vancomycin doses for CNS penetration (15 mg/kg q6h)
- Ceftriaxone 50 mg/kg q12h (max 2g per dose)

**Board Pearl**: "Empiric meningitis = ALWAYS vancomycin + ceftriaxone (never ceftriaxone alone)"

</details>

### Card 24: Fusobacterium - Lemierre Syndrome

**FRONT**: A 17-year-old with severe pharyngitis develops neck swelling, high fevers, and septic pulmonary emboli. What organism and syndrome should you suspect?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Fusobacterium necrophorum** causing **Lemierre Syndrome**

**Lemierre Syndrome**:
- Severe pharyngitis
- Internal jugular vein thrombophlebitis
- Septic emboli (usually to lungs)
- Also called "postanginal sepsis"

**Pathophysiology**:
1. Fusobacterium pharyngitis (or peritonsillar abscess)
2. Local invasion → internal jugular vein
3. Septic thrombophlebitis
4. Septic emboli → lungs (cavitary lesions), other organs
5. Severe sepsis

**Organism**:
- **Fusobacterium necrophorum** (anaerobe)
- Normal oral flora
- Can cause severe invasive disease

**Treatment**:
- **Antibiotics**: Metronidazole OR Clindamycin OR Ampicillin-sulbactam
- **Duration**: Prolonged (4-6 weeks)
- **Anticoagulation**: Controversial (case-by-case basis)
- **Surgical drainage**: If abscess present

**Imaging**:
- CT neck with IV contrast (internal jugular vein thrombosis)
- Chest CT (septic pulmonary emboli)

**Board Pearl**: "Severe pharyngitis + neck swelling + septic emboli = Lemierre syndrome (Fusobacterium necrophorum)"

</details>

### Card 25: Pseudomonas in Pediatrics - When to Cover

**FRONT**: When should you include Pseudomonas aeruginosa coverage in your empiric antibiotic regimen?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Healthcare-Associated Infections + Specific Host Factors**

**When to Cover Pseudomonas**:
1. **Healthcare-associated infections**
   - Prolonged hospitalization
   - Recent broad-spectrum antibiotics
   - Indwelling devices (catheters, tracheostomy)

2. **Immunocompromised hosts**
   - Neutropenic fever
   - Cystic fibrosis
   - Severe burns
   - Malignancy on chemotherapy

3. **Specific infections**
   - Cystic fibrosis pulmonary exacerbations
   - Severe burns
   - Nosocomial pneumonia (especially ventilator-associated)
   - Complicated UTI with healthcare exposure

**Anti-Pseudomonal Antibiotics**:
- **Cefepime** (4th generation cephalosporin)
- **Piperacillin-tazobactam**
- **Meropenem** (carbapenem)
- **Ciprofloxacin** (fluoroquinolone)
- **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin)

**Not Covered By**:
- Ceftriaxone (3rd generation cephalosporin)
- Ertapenem (carbapenem without Pseudomonas coverage)
- Oral cephalosporins

**Board Pearl**: "Pseudomonas = healthcare-associated + immunocompromised + CF. Need cefepime, pip-tazo, or meropenem."

</details>

### Card 26: Enterococcus in Neonatal UTI

**FRONT**: Why is ampicillin included in the empiric regimen for neonatal UTI when cephalosporins would cover most Gram-negatives?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Enterococcus is NOT covered by cephalosporins**

**Why Ampicillin is Essential**:
- Enterococcus is an important cause of neonatal UTI
- Cephalosporins have NO enterococcal coverage
- Ampicillin covers both Enterococcus AND Group B Strep
- This is why "Ampicillin + Gentamicin" (or Cefotaxime) is used

**Neonatal UTI Pathogens**:
- E. coli (most common)
- Enterococcus (more common in neonates than older children)
- Klebsiella
- Other Gram-negatives

**Empiric Regimen**:
- **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** OR
- **Ampicillin + Cefotaxime**

**What Each Drug Covers**:
- **Ampicillin**: GBS, Enterococcus
- **Gentamicin**: E. coli, Gram-negatives (synergy with ampicillin for Enterococcus)
- **Cefotaxime**: E. coli, Klebsiella, Gram-negatives (NOT enterococcus)

**Board Pearl**: "Never use cephalosporin alone for neonatal UTI - you'll miss Enterococcus. Always include ampicillin."

</details>

### Card 27: Meningococcal Prophylaxis - Who Needs It?

**FRONT**: A 5-year-old is diagnosed with meningococcal meningitis. Who needs chemoprophylaxis?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Close Contacts** within 7 days of symptom onset

**Who Needs Prophylaxis**:
1. **Household contacts**
2. **Daycare/school contacts** (close, prolonged exposure)
3. **Anyone with direct exposure to patient's oral secretions**
   - Kissing contact
   - Shared drinks/food
   - Mouth-to-mouth resuscitation
4. **Healthcare workers**:
   - ONLY if unprotected airway management (intubation without mask)
   - Routine care does NOT require prophylaxis

**Who Does NOT Need Prophylaxis**:
- Casual classroom contact
- Healthcare workers with standard PPE
- Brief social contact

**Prophylaxis Regimens**:
- **Rifampin**: 10 mg/kg PO BID × 2 days (max 600 mg/dose)
- **Ciprofloxacin**: Single dose 20 mg/kg PO (max 500 mg)
- **Ceftriaxone**: Single dose 125 mg IM (<15 years) or 250 mg IM (≥15 years)

**Timing**: Within 24 hours of index case diagnosis (ideally)

**Important**: **NO prophylaxis for pneumococcal meningitis** (different organism, no routine prophylaxis indicated)

**Board Pearl**: "Meningococcal disease = public health emergency. Close contacts need prophylaxis within 24 hours."

</details>

### Card 28: Mastoiditis - AOM Complication

**FRONT**: What bacterial pathogens cause acute mastoiditis? How does this relate to acute otitis media?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: Same organisms as AOM + Staph aureus

**Mastoiditis Pathogens**:
1. **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (most common)
2. **Staphylococcus aureus** (including MRSA)
3. **Group A Streptococcus**
4. **H. influenzae** (less common)

**Connection to AOM**:
- Mastoiditis is a **complication of inadequately treated AOM**
- Infection spreads from middle ear to mastoid air cells
- Can occur even with appropriate AOM treatment (less common)

**Clinical Features**:
- Post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness
- Protrusion of auricle
- Fever
- Ear pain
- Possible tympanic membrane perforation

**Empiric Therapy**:
- **Ceftriaxone** ± **Vancomycin** (if MRSA suspected)
- Add **Metronidazole** if chronic mastoiditis (anaerobic component)

**Management**:
- IV antibiotics (2-4 weeks)
- May require myringotomy (tympanostomy tube placement)
- Mastoidectomy if not improving

**Board Pearl**: "Mastoiditis = AOM complication. Same organisms (Pneumo, Staph, GAS) + need for surgical intervention."

</details>

### Card 29: Orbital Cellulitis - Ophthalmologic Emergency

**FRONT**: How do you distinguish preseptal cellulitis from orbital cellulitis? What are the most likely bacterial pathogens?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**:

**Preseptal (Periorbital) Cellulitis**:
- Infection **anterior to orbital septum**
- Eyelid swelling, erythema
- **NO vision changes**
- **NO ophthalmoplegia** (eye movement normal)
- **NO proptosis**
- Less severe

**Orbital (Postseptal) Cellulitis**:
- Infection **posterior to orbital septum**
- **Vision changes** (decreased acuity, pain with eye movement)
- **Ophthalmoplegia** (restricted eye movement)
- **Proptosis** (eye protrusion)
- **EMERGENCY** (risk of vision loss, intracranial extension)

**Pathogens** (Often from sinusitis extension):
- **Streptococcus pneumoniae**
- **Staphylococcus aureus** (including MRSA)
- **Streptococcus anginosus group**
- Polymicrobial in some cases

**Empiric Therapy**:
- **Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin** OR
- **Ampicillin-sulbactam** (± Vancomycin)

**Management**:
- Ophthalmology consultation URGENT
- CT orbits/sinuses (rule out abscess)
- Surgical drainage if subperiosteal or orbital abscess

**Board Pearl**: "Orbital cellulitis = vision changes + ophthalmoplegia + proptosis = EMERGENCY. IV antibiotics + ophthalmology consult."

</details>

### Card 30: Necrotizing Fasciitis - Surgical Emergency

**FRONT**: A patient with rapidly progressive SSTI has severe pain out of proportion to exam, systemic toxicity, and skin necrosis. What diagnosis should you suspect? What organisms cause it?

<details>
<summary>Click for BACK</summary>

**ANSWER**: **Necrotizing Fasciitis** (Flesh-Eating Disease)

**Two Types**:

**Type 1 (Polymicrobial)**:
- Aerobic + Anaerobic bacteria
- **Staph aureus + Anaerobes** (Bacteroides, Clostridium, Peptostreptococcus)
- More common in adults with comorbidities (diabetes, obesity)
- Post-surgical, post-trauma

**Type 2 (Monomicrobial)**:
- **Group A Streptococcus** (S. pyogenes)
- Exotoxin-mediated (Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome)
- Can occur in previously healthy individuals
- Community-acquired

**Clinical Features** (RED FLAGS):
- **Pain out of proportion to exam** (early sign)
- Rapidly progressive
- Systemic toxicity (fever, hypotension, altered mental status)
- Skin changes: dusky, bullae, necrosis, crepitus
- **SURGICAL EMERGENCY**

**Treatment**:
- **URGENT surgical debridement** (definitive therapy)
- **Broad-spectrum antibiotics**:
  - **Vancomycin** (MRSA coverage)
  - **Piperacillin-tazobactam** OR **Meropenem** (Gram-negative + anaerobic)
  - **Clindamycin** (toxin suppression for GAS)
- **ICU support** (often multi-organ failure)

**Board Pearl**: "Necrotizing fasciitis = pain >> exam findings = SURGICAL EMERGENCY. Broad antibiotics + urgent debridement."

</details>
